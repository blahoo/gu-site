Sometimes I think about dropping out of school and becoming a professional poker player. Not because I hate learning, but because the education system feels fundamentally broken for people who think differently.

## The Opportunity Cost

While I'm sitting in classes learning about things I could Google in 30 seconds, I could be building companies, creating products, or yes - making money at poker tables.

The question isn't whether I can learn calculus. The question is whether spending 4 years in school is the best use of my time when I could be learning by doing.

## The Poker Parallel

Poker rewards:
- Risk assessment
- Pattern recognition
- Emotional control
- Quick decision-making
- Learning from failure

Sound like entrepreneurship? That's because it is.

## The Real Education

The most valuable lessons I've learned haven't come from textbooks:
- Building things teaches you more than studying them
- Failure is the best teacher
- Money is just a way to keep score
- The best education is the one you give yourself

## The Safety Net Myth

People say "finish school, you need something to fall back on."

But what if the safety net is actually a trap? What if knowing you have a backup plan makes you less likely to go all-in on what you really want to do?

## The Decision

I probably won't drop out. But the fact that I'm even considering it says something about how we think about education, risk, and the value of conventional paths.

Maybe the real lesson is that the best decisions aren't always the safe ones.


# What Poker Taught Me About Engineering

![Poker Table](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1606092195730-5d7b9af1efc5?w=600&h=400&fit=crop)

Sitting at a poker table for hours teaches you things about decision-making, risk management, and human psychology that no engineering textbook ever could.

## Expected Value vs. Outcome

In poker, you can make the right decision and still lose. In engineering, you can build the perfect system and still have it fail in production.

Both teach you to focus on process over results.

## Information Management

Poker players work with incomplete information and have to make optimal decisions anyway. Sound familiar?

Every engineering project is the same - you never have all the data you want, but you still need to ship.

## Risk vs. Reward

The best poker players aren't the ones who avoid risk - they're the ones who take calculated risks when the odds are in their favor.

Same with building things. The biggest mistake isn't taking risks, it's not taking enough of the right ones.

## Reading the Room

Poker teaches you to read people, to understand their motivations and predict their actions.

In engineering, your "opponents" are physics, complexity, and time. But the principles are the same - pay attention, adapt your strategy, and always be thinking one step ahead.

## The Grind

Both poker and engineering reward consistency over brilliance. Show up every day, make good decisions, and trust the process.

The flashy moves get the attention, but it's the fundamentals that pay the bills.
